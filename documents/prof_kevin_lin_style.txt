What is Professor Kevin Lin's teaching style, grading rubric structure, and assignment difficulty for data structures and computer science courses?

Professor Kevin Lin is known for having an excellent, engaging, and dynamic oration style during lectures. In introductory or data-intensive settings, he frequently incorporates challenging live coding examples and provides highly practical programming tricks. However, his upper-level courses, such as data structures (CSE 373), present a significantly stressful and polarizing experience for many students.

Course Structure and Grading Mechanics:
- Evaluation Layout: The grading system is heavily test-heavy, frequently utilizing exams as the primary metric for a student's final score. While daily participation, attendance, and coding projects require an immense time commitment from students, they often contribute comparatively little to the final numeric grade. 
- Rubric Clarity: He does not use a conventional grading rubric. Students frequently describe the scale as overly complicated, avant-garde, or arbitrary, which can make navigating expectations highly confusing. 
- Lecture Expectations: His classroom strategy leans heavily toward an active-learning or flipped style. Lecture periods are frequently dedicated to practicing actual exam problems rather than introducing core definitions. As a result, students must independently learn and fully grasp the theoretical computer science concepts before stepping into the classroom.

Assignment and Classroom Environment:
- Project Specifications: Software engineering assignments are noted for being structurally confusing, dense, and difficult to parse without external guidance.
- Behavioral Expectations: He maintains an incredibly strict stance on academic integrity and code style guidelines, which creates a high-stakes environment regarding student misconduct and original code execution.
